The puzzleWhy do some countries trade billions, and others almost nothing?

There is an old answer borrowed from physics, called the gravity model. Two bodies attract in proportion to their masses and in inverse proportion to the distance between them. Swap mass for economic size and the analogy holds up well for trade: big economies trade more, and distant economies trade less. Regression puts exact numbers on "more" and "less," and lets us check whether the story survives contact with data.

$$\ln(\text{trade}) = a + b_1\ln(\text{distance}) + b_2\ln(\text{GDP}_{\text{exporter}}) + b_3\ln(\text{GDP}_{\text{importer}}) + \varepsilon$$

Because the variables are in logs, each slope is an elasticity: the percent change in trade for a one percent change in the predictor. We build the model in three passes, adding one idea at a time.

The arcThree models, and what each one adds

Pass 1 · Simple

Distance only

0.06R-squared

The distance elasticity is a real $-0.81$ with a sharp t-stat, yet it explains just 6 percent of why trade volumes differ. One variable is rarely the story.

Pass 2 · Multiple

Add both economies' size

0.35R-squared

Both GDP elasticities land near 1, exactly what gravity predicts. R-squared jumps sixfold. Size, not distance, was the missing piece.

Pass 3 · Dummies

Add border, language, treaty

0.41R-squared

A trade agreement goes with roughly +208 percent more trade. A surprising negative sign on borders becomes a lesson in itself.

ProvenanceWhere the data comes from

Trade values: BACI 2022 (CEPII), built on UN Comtrade. Distance, contiguity, common language, colonial history, and trade-agreement membership: the CEPII Gravity database. GDP: the World Bank's World Development Indicators. Each row is one directed exporter to importer pair. The full column-by-column dictionary lives in data/DATA_DICTIONARY.md, and the whole workbook rebuilds from build_workbook.py.
